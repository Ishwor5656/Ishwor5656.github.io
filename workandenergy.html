Work and Energy

A powerful alternative approach to mechanics that deals with the currency of the universe: energy.

The Language of Energy

Understanding how energy is transferred and transformed.

Work

In physics, work is done on an object when a force causes a displacement. It is the transfer of energy. For a constant force, work is calculated as W = Fd cos(θ), where θ is the angle between the force and displacement vectors.

  • Positive Work: Force has a component in the direction of motion (speeds up).
  • Negative Work: Force has a component opposite the direction of motion (slows down).
  • Zero Work: Force is perpendicular to motion (e.g., carrying a box horizontally).

Kinetic Energy (KE)

This is the energy of motion. Any object that is moving has kinetic energy. It depends on both the mass of the object and the square of its speed, meaning speed has a much larger impact on KE.

KE = ½mv²

Potential Energy (PE)

This is stored energy due to an object's position or configuration. The most common type is Gravitational Potential Energy, which depends on an object's mass, height, and the strength of gravity.

PE_gravity = mgh

Work-Energy Theorem & Conservation

The Work-Energy Theorem states that the net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy (W_net = ΔKE).

The Law of Conservation of Energy is one of the most fundamental principles in physics. It states that in an isolated system, the total mechanical energy (KE + PE) remains constant if only conservative forces (like gravity) are doing work.

Power

Power is the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred. It tells you how quickly work is being performed. The SI unit for power is the Watt (W), which is one Joule per second.

P = Work / time = W / t

Test Your Knowledge

Apply the principles of work and energy.

1. A waiter carries a heavy tray of food horizontally across a room at a constant velocity. Is he doing any work on the tray?

Show Answer

Answer: No. In physics, for work to be done, the force must cause a displacement. The waiter applies an upward force to counteract gravity, but the displacement is horizontal. Since the force is perpendicular (90 degrees) to the displacement, cos(90°) = 0, so no work is done.

2. Two cars, one twice as heavy as the other, are driving at the same speed. Which car has more kinetic energy?

Show Answer

Answer: The heavier car. Kinetic energy (KE = ½mv²) is directly proportional to mass. If the speed is the same, the car with twice the mass will have twice the kinetic energy.

3. A satellite is in a perfectly circular orbit around the Earth. Does the Earth's gravity do any work on the satellite?

Show Answer

Answer: No. The force of gravity is always directed towards the center of the Earth, while the satellite's instantaneous velocity (and displacement) is tangent to the orbit. The force is always perpendicular to the displacement, so no work is done by gravity.

4. Can kinetic energy ever be negative? Can potential energy ever be negative?

Show Answer

Answer: Kinetic energy (½mv²) can never be negative because mass and v² are always non-negative. Potential energy can be negative. It depends on where you define the zero-point (h=0). If an object is below this reference point, its gravitational potential energy is negative.

5. A person runs up a flight of stairs. A second person walks up the same stairs. Who does more work? Who uses more power?

Show Answer

Answer: They both do the same amount of work, as they have the same mass (assuming they are identical) and move through the same vertical displacement (W = mgh). However, the runner uses more power because they do the work in less time (P = W/t).

6. What happens to the total mechanical energy of a system if only conservative forces are acting?

Show Answer

Answer: It remains constant. This is the principle of conservation of mechanical energy. Energy can transform between kinetic and potential forms (like a pendulum swinging), but the total amount (KE + PE) does not change.

7. How does friction affect the total mechanical energy of a system?

Show Answer

Answer: Friction is a non-conservative force. It does negative work, converting mechanical energy into thermal energy (heat). Therefore, friction causes the total mechanical energy of a system to decrease.

8. If you push a box twice as far while applying the same force, how much more work do you do?

Show Answer

Answer: You do twice as much work. Work (W = Fd) is directly proportional to the distance. If you double the distance, you double the work.

9. A simple pendulum is swinging back and forth. At which point in its swing is its kinetic energy maximum? At which point is its potential energy maximum?

Show Answer

Answer: Kinetic energy is maximum at the lowest point of the swing, where the pendulum is moving fastest. Potential energy is maximum at the highest points of the swing (the endpoints), where it momentarily stops before changing direction.

10. According to the Work-Energy Theorem, what must be true for an object's speed to increase?

Show Answer

Answer: The net work done on the object must be positive. A positive net work leads to a positive change in kinetic energy (ΔKE), which means the final kinetic energy is greater than the initial, and therefore the speed has increased.

1. A 10 kg box is lifted 2 meters straight up. How much work is done? (Use g = 9.8 m/s²)

Show Answer

Answer: 196 Joules. The force required is the box's weight, F = mg = 10 kg * 9.8 m/s² = 98 N. Work W = Fd = 98 N * 2 m.

2. A 1500 kg car is traveling at 20 m/s. What is its kinetic energy?

Show Answer

Answer: 300,000 Joules (or 300 kJ). Use KE = ½mv² = 0.5 * 1500 kg * (20 m/s)².

3. A 5 kg ball is on a shelf 3 meters above the ground. What is its gravitational potential energy relative to the ground?

Show Answer

Answer: 147 Joules. Use PE = mgh = 5 kg * 9.8 m/s² * 3 m.

4. A net force of 1000 N acts on a 500 kg car, initially at rest, over a distance of 50 m. What is the car's final kinetic energy?

Show Answer

Answer: 50,000 Joules. By the Work-Energy Theorem, W_net = ΔKE. W_net = Fd = 1000 N * 50 m = 50,000 J. Since it started from rest, KE_initial = 0, so ΔKE = KE_final.

5. A 2 kg rock is dropped from a height of 10 m. What is its speed just before it hits the ground (ignoring air resistance)?

Show Answer

Answer: 14 m/s. Use conservation of energy: PE_initial + KE_initial = PE_final + KE_final. mgh + 0 = 0 + ½mv². The mass cancels out. gh = ½v². v = sqrt(2gh) = sqrt(2 * 9.8 * 10).

6. An engine does 2400 J of work in 8 seconds. What is its power output?

Show Answer

Answer: 300 Watts. Use P = W / t = 2400 J / 8 s.

7. A person pushes a 40 kg crate 5 m along a horizontal floor with a force of 150 N. A friction force of 70 N opposes the motion. What is the net work done on the crate?

Show Answer

Answer: 400 Joules. The net force is F_net = F_push - f = 150 N - 70 N = 80 N. The net work is W_net = F_net * d = 80 N * 5 m.

8. Using the result from the previous question, if the crate started from rest, what is its final speed?

Show Answer

Answer: 4.47 m/s. W_net = ΔKE = ½mv_f² - 0. So, 400 J = 0.5 * 40 kg * v_f². v_f = sqrt(400 / 20).

9. A 60 kg skateboarder starts from rest at the top of a 5 m high ramp. What is her speed at the bottom, assuming no friction?

Show Answer

Answer: 9.9 m/s. Use conservation of energy: PE_top = KE_bottom. mgh = ½mv². The mass cancels. v = sqrt(2gh) = sqrt(2 * 9.8 * 5).

10. A 100 Watt light bulb is left on for 1 hour (3600 s). How much energy does it consume?

Show Answer

Answer: 360,000 Joules (or 360 kJ). Energy (Work) = Power * time = 100 W * 3600 s.

Key Tips for Work and Energy

1. Is Energy Conserved?

This is the first question to ask. If there are no non-conservative forces (like friction, air resistance, or an applied push/pull), then you can use the powerful conservation of energy equation (KEi + PEi = KEf + PEf).

2. Use the Work-Energy Theorem

If energy is NOT conserved, the Work-Energy theorem is your best tool. The net work done (including work by friction) is equal to the change in kinetic energy. W_net = ΔKE.

3. Set Your Zero Point

For potential energy problems, you can choose where h=0. A smart choice (like the lowest point in the problem) can simplify your calculations by making one of the PE terms zero.

4. Watch the Angle in Work

Remember that work depends on the angle between force and displacement (W = Fd cos(θ)). A force perpendicular to motion does no work. A force opposite to motion does negative work.
