Zeroth Law & Thermal Equilibrium

A focused look at the fundamental concept that underpins all of thermodynamics: thermal equilibrium and the law that defines temperature.

1. Core Concepts & Theory

Thermal Equilibrium

Two systems are in thermal equilibrium if there is no net flow of thermal energy between them when they are connected by a path permeable to heat. In simple terms, this means they are at the same temperature. Even though microscopic energy exchange may still occur, the overall macroscopic properties (like pressure, volume) do not change over time.

The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics

The Zeroth Law provides the formal definition of temperature. It states:

If system A is in thermal equilibrium with system C, and system B is also in thermal equilibrium with system C, then systems A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other.

This may seem obvious, but it is a crucial axiom. It establishes that temperature is a fundamental, measurable property of a system. The "system C" in this law is effectively a thermometer.

5. Key Insights

  • Equilibrium = Same Temperature: The core meaning of thermal equilibrium is that objects have the same temperature, and there is no *net* heat flow between them.
  • Zeroth Law Defines Temperature: The main purpose of the Zeroth Law is to make "temperature" a valid scientific concept that can be reliably measured.
  • Transitive Property: The law is a statement of the transitive property of thermal equilibrium: If A=C and B=C, then A=B.
  • Equilibrium is Dynamic: At the microscopic level, atoms and molecules are still exchanging energy, but the overall rates of exchange are equal, leading to no macroscopic change.
