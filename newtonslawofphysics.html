Newton's Laws of Motion

The three pillars of classical mechanics that describe the relationship between an object and the forces acting upon it.

I. The Law of Inertia

"An object at rest stays at rest and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and in the same direction unless acted upon by an unbalanced force."

This law is all about inertia - the resistance of any physical object to any change in its state of motion. Essentially, things keep doing what they're doing unless a force interferes.

Example: A hockey puck on frictionless ice will continue to slide forever. A soccer ball on a field eventually stops because of the unbalanced force of friction.

II. The Law of Acceleration

"The acceleration of an object as produced by a net force is directly proportional to the magnitude of the net force, in the same direction as the net force, and inversely proportional to the mass of the object."

This is famously summarized by the equation F = ma. It's the link between force, mass, and acceleration.

Example: It's easier to push an empty shopping cart (less mass) than a full one. To make either cart accelerate faster, you must push it with more force.

III. The Law of Action-Reaction

"For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."

This law states that forces always occur in pairs. If object A pushes on object B, object B pushes back on object A with the same force. These forces act on *different* objects.

Example: When you jump, your legs push down on the ground. The ground then pushes up on you with an equal force, propelling you into the air.

Test Your Knowledge

Engage with concepts, solve problems, and master the laws.

1. If you are in a car that is struck from behind, you can suffer a whiplash injury. How does Newton's law of inertia explain this?

Show Answer

Answer: Your body is pushed forward by the car seat, but your head, due to inertia, tends to stay in its original position. This causes your head to snap back relative to your body, leading to whiplash.

2. Can an object be in motion if the net force acting on it is zero? Explain.

Show Answer

Answer: Yes. According to the first law, if the net force is zero, an object in motion will stay in motion with a constant velocity (constant speed and direction). A car on cruise control on a straight road is a good approximation.

3. A bug and the windshield of a fast-moving car collide. Which of them experiences a greater impact force? Which experiences greater acceleration?

Show Answer

Answer: According to the third law, the forces are equal and opposite. However, according to the second law (a = F/m), the bug, having a much smaller mass, experiences a tremendously larger acceleration.

4. Why do you have to push harder on the pedals of a bicycle to start it moving than to keep it moving?

Show Answer

Answer: This is due to inertia (first law) and static friction. You must apply enough force to overcome the bike's inertia (its resistance to changing from rest to motion) and the higher static friction. Once moving, you only need to overcome kinetic friction, which is typically lower.

5. A rocket expels gas in a downward direction to move upward. How does this demonstrate the law of action-reaction?

Show Answer

Answer: The rocket exerts a downward force on the gas (action). In response, the gas exerts an equal and opposite upward force on the rocket (reaction), causing it to accelerate upwards.

6. If an elephant and a feather are dropped in a vacuum, they fall at the same rate. How does Newton's second law (F=ma) explain this, considering gravity pulls much harder on the elephant?

Show Answer

Answer: The force of gravity (F) on the elephant is much larger, but its mass (m) is also much larger. The ratio F/m (which equals acceleration, a) ends up being the same for both objects. The larger force is perfectly compensated by the larger inertia.

7. Is it possible for an object to be traveling westward and simultaneously have a net force on it directed eastward? If so, give an example.

Show Answer

Answer: Yes. This happens when an object is decelerating (slowing down). For example, a car traveling westward that applies its brakes has a net force (from friction) directed eastward, causing it to slow down.

8. When you walk, you push backward on the ground. What force actually pushes you forward?

Show Answer

Answer: According to the third law, as you push backward on the ground (action), the ground pushes forward on you with an equal and opposite force (reaction). This forward force from the ground is what propels you.

9. Why is it more difficult to do a pull-up than it is to just hang from a bar?

Show Answer

Answer: When hanging, your muscles exert a force equal to your weight to keep you stationary (zero net force). To do a pull-up, you must exert a force greater than your weight to create a net upward force, causing an upward acceleration (second law).

10. A book is sitting at rest on a table. What is the "reaction" force to the gravitational force pulling the book down?

Show Answer

Answer: This is a classic trick question. The reaction force is NOT the table pushing up. The gravitational force is the Earth pulling the book down (action). Therefore, the reaction force is the book pulling the Earth up with an equal and opposite gravitational force.

1. A force of 20 N is applied to a 5 kg box. What is the acceleration of the box?

Show Answer

Answer: 4 m/s² (Using F=ma, a = F/m = 20N / 5kg)

2. A 1500 kg car accelerates from rest to 25 m/s in 10 seconds. What is the net force acting on the car?

Show Answer

Answer: 3750 N (First find acceleration: a = Δv/t = 25m/s / 10s = 2.5 m/s². Then use F=ma = 1500kg * 2.5m/s²)

3. A 60 kg person stands on a scale in an elevator. What does the scale read if the elevator is accelerating upwards at 2 m/s²? (Use g = 9.8 m/s²)

Show Answer

Answer: 708 N (The scale reads the normal force. F_net = N - mg = ma. So, N = mg + ma = 60kg * (9.8m/s² + 2m/s²) )

4. A 0.5 kg ball is thrown with a force of 100 N. What is its initial acceleration?

Show Answer

Answer: 200 m/s² (a = F/m = 100N / 0.5kg)

5. Two boxes, 4 kg and 6 kg, are connected by a string and pulled by a force of 30 N on a frictionless surface. What is the acceleration of the system?

Show Answer

Answer: 3 m/s² (Treat as one system. Total mass = 10 kg. a = F_total / m_total = 30N / 10kg)

6. What is the weight of a 75 kg astronaut on the Moon, where the gravitational acceleration is 1.62 m/s²?

Show Answer

Answer: 121.5 N (Weight = mass * gravitational acceleration = 75kg * 1.62 m/s²)

7. A 10 kg block is pushed with 50 N of force, but a friction force of 10 N opposes it. What is the block's acceleration?

Show Answer

Answer: 4 m/s² (F_net = F_applied - F_friction = 50N - 10N = 40N. Then a = F_net / m = 40N / 10kg)

8. A 2 kg object moving at 5 m/s is brought to rest in 2 seconds. What force was required?

Show Answer

Answer: -5 N (The negative sign indicates the force opposes the motion. a = (0-5)/2 = -2.5 m/s². F = 2kg * -2.5m/s²)

9. A 70 kg skateboarder pushes off a wall with a force of 35 N. What is the skateboarder's initial acceleration?

Show Answer

Answer: 0.5 m/s² (a = F/m = 35N / 70kg)

10. A 1 kg bird is flying. If it pushes down on the air with a force of 12 N, what is its upward acceleration? (Use g = 9.8 m/s²)

Show Answer

Answer: 2.2 m/s² (F_net = F_lift - Weight = 12N - (1kg * 9.8m/s²) = 2.2 N. Then a = F_net / m = 2.2N / 1kg)

Key Tips for Success

1. Isolate the System

Always start by clearly defining the object or system you are analyzing. Draw a free-body diagram to identify ALL forces acting on that object.

2. Net Force is Key

Remember that F in F=ma is the *net* (or total) force. You must sum up all the forces (considering their directions) before calculating acceleration.

3. Forces Come in Pairs

For every force, there's a reaction force. Make sure you don't mix them up. The action and reaction forces act on *different* objects.

4. Mass vs. Weight

Mass is the amount of 'stuff' in an object (kg) and is constant everywhere. Weight is the force of gravity on that mass (N) and changes depending on the planet.
