1. Core Concepts & Theory

1.1 Linear Momentum

Momentum (vector) is defined as p = m v. Units: kg·m/s. It encodes both magnitude and direction of motion and is additive for systems.

Principle: In an isolated system (no net external impulse), total momentum is conserved: Σp_before = Σp_after.

1.2 Impulse & Momentum Change

Impulse J = ∫ F dt = Δp. Large momentum change can result from a large force over a small time or a moderate force over longer time. Increasing impact time (e.g., airbags) reduces peak force.

1.3 System & Internal Forces

Internal forces always form action–reaction pairs and cancel in the vector sum across the system. Only external forces alter the system’s total momentum.

1.4 Types of Collisions

  • Perfectly Elastic: Momentum and Kinetic Energy (KE) both conserved.
  • Perfectly Inelastic: Bodies stick. Momentum conserved, KE not; KE loss is maximum for given initial conditions.
  • Partially Inelastic: Momentum conserved, some KE lost. Characterized by coefficient of restitution e.

1.5 Coefficient of Restitution

e = (relative speed of separation)/(relative speed of approach) along line of impact. For perfectly elastic e = 1; perfectly inelastic e = 0.

1.6 Center of Mass (COM) Frame

Analyzing collisions in the COM frame often simplifies calculations. In elastic collisions, speeds of particles are symmetric about COM in pre/post states (they reverse directions with same magnitudes).

1.7 2D & Oblique Collisions

Momentum is conserved as a vector: conserve each component independently. Often one uses coordinate axes aligned and perpendicular to line of impact. The impulse acts along the line of centers (for spherical bodies), so perpendicular components of relative velocity are unchanged (in elastic collisions).

1.8 Explosion / Recoil

Internal energy converts (e.g., chemical) into kinetic energy. Momentum still conserved; objects move in opposite directions so vector sum is zero if initial COM was at rest.

1.9 Sequential Collisions & Energy Cascade

Multiple collisions in a line (e.g., Newton’s cradle) propagate impulse. Slight energy losses accumulate, making real setups damp with time.

1.10 Advanced Considerations

  • Relativistic Momentum: p = γ m v at high speeds.
  • Angular Momentum Coupling: Off-center impacts generate torque → rotational KE.
  • Deformable Bodies: Energy partition includes internal modes (heat, sound).

5. Strategic Tips & Exam Insights

  • Vector First: Always write momentum conservation component-wise before plugging numbers.
  • Classify Collision: Identify type early to know which quantities remain conserved.
  • Check Feasibility: Momentum + KE must be simultaneously satisfiable; contradictions flag bad data.
  • COM Frame Trick: In elastic cases, relative velocities invert → simplified relations.
  • Impulse Axis: Only components along line of impact change (for smooth, central collisions).
  • Restitution Equation: Often faster than applying KE conservation first.
  • Sequential Collisions: Update velocities after each impact; test for re-collisions.
  • Design Insight: Increase impact time (Δt) to reduce force; mention in safety answers.
  • Dimensional Filter: Eliminate impossible answers quickly with units.
  • Energy Accounting: Track where “lost” KE goes (internal, heat, sound).

6. Quick Summary

Momentum & collisions rely on universal momentum conservation. Classification (elastic/inelastic/super-elastic) determines kinetic energy treatment, while impulse links force and momentum change. COM frame symmetry simplifies elastic analysis; restitution quantifies ‘bounciness’. Rigorous consistency checks prevent acceptance of impossible data, and practical design leverages Δt extension to mitigate forces. Mastery comes from blending algebraic conservation with physical intuition about energy channels and reference frames.
