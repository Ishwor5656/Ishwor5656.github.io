Temperature, Heat & Expansion

Deep Dive • Practice • Mastery

🌡️ Temperature

Temperature is a macroscopic indicator of the average random translational kinetic energy of microscopic particles. Scales: Celsius, Kelvin, Fahrenheit. Absolute zero (0 K) is the theoretical lower bound; quantum zero‑point energy remains.

🔥 Heat

Heat is energy in transit due solely to temperature difference; once transferred it becomes internal energy. Modes: Conduction, Convection, Radiation.

⚡ Specific Heat Capacity

Specific heat capacity c (J·kg⁻¹·K⁻¹) relates heat and temperature change: Q = m c ΔT. Large c → thermal buffering (water); small c → rapid response (metals).

💧 Water’s High Specific Heat

Hydrogen bonding absorbs energy altering structure before kinetic energy rises → large c ≈ 4200 J·kg⁻¹·K⁻¹. Effects: moderated climate, biological temperature stability, ocean heat storage.

📏 Thermal Expansion

For moderate ΔT: ΔL = α L₀ ΔT; area ≈ 2α; volume ≈ 3α (isotropic). Constraining expansion produces stress σ ≈ E α ΔT and elastic energy density ≈ ½E(αΔT)².

🧠 Conceptual Questions (20 Expanded)

Expanded

Click to reveal detailed explanations.

1. Why does temperature reflect only average translational kinetic energy and not total internal energy?
Internal energy includes kinetic (translational, rotational, vibrational) plus potential components. Temperature is tied to the average translational kinetic energy that equalizes via collisions and sets heat flow direction; other portions can vary independently.
2. Two blocks (Al & steel) same temperature yet different internal energies—why?
Different heat capacities and masses yield different total internal energies. Equal temperature just means identical average kinetic energy per active DOF, not identical total energy.
3. Metal feels colder than wood at same T: role of thermal effusivity.
Metal’s high effusivity √(kρc) pulls heat rapidly from skin lowering local skin temperature; wood’s low k limits transient flux so it feels warmer.
4. Heat vs work vs internal energy in a piston expansion.
Heat Q enters due to ΔT; part raises internal energy U; part emerges as work W = PΔV. U is state; Q & W are path processes. ΔU = Q − W (system sign).
5. How water’s high c moderates coastal climates.
Large c and mass: for same solar input ΔT is small; stored energy released slowly at night reducing swings.
6. Steam burn vs 100 °C water burn severity.
Steam releases latent heat of condensation plus sensible cooling; latent term dominates energy delivered to tissue.
7. Need for expansion joints in bridges/rails.
Avoid restrained thermal strain building stress σ≈EαΔT causing buckling or cracking.
8. Bimetal strip bending direction.
Higher‑α layer expands more; composite curves with that layer outward (longer arc).
9. Derive 2α & 3α approximate coefficients.
(1+αΔT)²≈1+2αΔT; (1+αΔT)³≈1+3αΔT for αΔT≪1; neglect higher powers.
10. Why Kelvin for gas laws.
Average kinetic energy ∝ absolute T; zero reference required for direct proportionality.
11. 0 °C object heating −10 °C object explanation.
Heat flows from higher T (0 °C) to lower (−10 °C) independent of subjective “cold”.
12. k vs c difference.
k: rate of heat transfer (flux). c: energy storage per mass per K. Independent material properties.
13. α constant only for small ΔT—why?
Anharmonic lattice causes α(T) variation; constant is first‑order linearization.
14. Water expansion on freezing & ecology.
Open hydrogen‑bond lattice reduces density so ice floats insulating water below.
15. Thermal shock mechanism in glass.
Surface expands quickly; interior lags → tensile stress exceeds strength. Mitigate with low‑α glass & gradual heating.
16. Specific heat c vs molar heat capacity Cm.
c per kg; Cm per mole revealing DOF pattern (monatomic vs diatomic). Useful for gas kinetic comparisons.
17. Large lakes delay seasonal extremes—why?
Large thermal mass (mc) & greater depth increase time constant for temperature changes relative to heat flux area.
18. Radiative exchange at equal T.
Mutual emission/absorption ongoing; net zero when T & emissivity match (dynamic equilibrium).
19. Volumetric expansion ≈3α limitation.
First-order of (1+αΔT)^3; fails for large αΔT or anisotropic materials.
20. Measuring α & key errors.
Dilatometer with controlled ramp; errors: gradients, calibration drift, reference expansion, sensor resolution, lag.
