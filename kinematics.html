Kinematics

The branch of mechanics concerned with the motion of objects without reference to the forces which cause the motion.

Core Concepts of Motion

Understanding the language used to describe how things move.

Distance vs. Displacement

Distance is a scalar quantity that refers to "how much ground an object has covered" during its motion.

Displacement is a vector quantity that refers to "how far out of place an object is"; it is the object's overall change in position.

Example: If you walk 5 meters east and then 5 meters west, your distance traveled is 10 meters, but your displacement is 0 meters because you ended up where you started.

Speed vs. Velocity

Speed is a scalar quantity that refers to "how fast an object is moving."

Velocity is a vector quantity that refers to "the rate at which an object changes its position." It includes direction.

Example: A car traveling at 60 km/h has a speed. A car traveling at 60 km/h due north has a velocity. You can have a constant speed while changing velocity (e.g., going in a circle).

Acceleration

Acceleration is a vector quantity defined as the rate at which an object changes its velocity. An object is accelerating if it is changing its velocity.

This can mean speeding up, slowing down (deceleration), or changing direction. A car turning a corner at a constant speed is still accelerating because its direction (and thus velocity) is changing.

The Kinematic Equations

For motion with constant acceleration, a set of powerful equations allows us to relate displacement (Δx), time (t), initial velocity (v₀), final velocity (v), and acceleration (a).

v = v₀ + at
Δx = v₀t + ½at²
v² = v₀² + 2aΔx

Test Your Knowledge

Apply the concepts to solidify your understanding.

1. Is it possible to have a constant speed but a changing velocity? Explain.

Show Answer

Answer: Yes. Velocity is a vector with both magnitude (speed) and direction. If you are moving in a circle at a constant speed, your direction is constantly changing, which means your velocity is changing, and you are accelerating.

2. A ball is thrown straight up into the air. What is its velocity and acceleration at the very top of its path?

Show Answer

Answer: At the peak of its path, the ball's instantaneous velocity is zero for a moment as it changes direction. However, its acceleration is constant throughout the flight: 9.8 m/s² downwards (due to gravity).

3. Can an object have a northward velocity and a southward acceleration? If so, what is happening to the object?

Show Answer

Answer: Yes. This means the object is moving north but slowing down. The acceleration is in the opposite direction to the velocity, causing deceleration.

4. If you drop a feather and a hammer on the Moon (which has no air resistance), which one hits the ground first?

Show Answer

Answer: They hit the ground at the same time. In the absence of air resistance, all objects fall with the same constant acceleration, regardless of their mass.

5. What does the slope of a position-time graph represent?

Show Answer

Answer: The slope of a position-time graph represents the object's velocity. A steep slope means a high velocity, and a horizontal line means the object is at rest (zero velocity).

6. What does the area under a velocity-time graph represent?

Show Answer

Answer: The area under a velocity-time graph represents the object's displacement. If the graph is below the x-axis, the area is negative, representing displacement in the negative direction.

7. Can an object have zero velocity but non-zero acceleration?

Show Answer

Answer: Yes. This occurs at the exact moment an object changes direction. For example, the ball thrown upwards has zero velocity at its highest point but still has an acceleration of 9.8 m/s² downwards.

8. A car is traveling west and rounds a curve to travel south. Does it accelerate?

Show Answer

Answer: Yes. Even if the car's speed is constant, its direction of motion changes, which means its velocity changes. A change in velocity is acceleration.

9. If an object has a constant, non-zero acceleration, is its velocity ever constant?

Show Answer

Answer: No. By definition, acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. If acceleration is non-zero, the velocity must be changing.

10. Imagine you are running in a straight line at a constant speed. Is your acceleration zero?

Show Answer

Answer: Yes. If both your speed and direction are constant, your velocity is constant. If your velocity is not changing, your acceleration is zero.

1. A car accelerates from rest to 30 m/s in 6 seconds. What is its acceleration?

Show Answer

Answer: 5 m/s². Use a = (v - v₀) / t. a = (30 m/s - 0 m/s) / 6 s.

2. A stone is dropped from a tall cliff. How far has it fallen after 4 seconds? (Use g = 9.8 m/s²)

Show Answer

Answer: 78.4 meters. Use Δx = v₀t + ½at². Since it's dropped, v₀ = 0. Δx = 0 + ½(9.8 m/s²)(4 s)².

3. A train slows down from 40 m/s to 10 m/s over a distance of 150 m. What is its acceleration?

Show Answer

Answer: -5 m/s². Use v² = v₀² + 2aΔx. (10² = 40² + 2 * a * 150). 100 = 1600 + 300a. -1500 = 300a.

4. An airplane needs to reach a speed of 75 m/s to take off. If the runway is 1500 m long, what is the minimum constant acceleration required?

Show Answer

Answer: 1.875 m/s². Use v² = v₀² + 2aΔx. (75² = 0² + 2 * a * 1500). 5625 = 3000a.

5. A ball is thrown vertically upward with an initial velocity of 25 m/s. How long does it take to reach its highest point? (Use g = 9.8 m/s²)

Show Answer

Answer: 2.55 seconds. At the highest point, v = 0. Use v = v₀ + at. (0 = 25 m/s + (-9.8 m/s²)t).

6. A car traveling at 20 m/s accelerates at 3 m/s² for 5 seconds. What is its final velocity?

Show Answer

Answer: 35 m/s. Use v = v₀ + at. v = 20 m/s + (3 m/s² * 5 s).

7. How far does the car in the previous question travel during those 5 seconds?

Show Answer

Answer: 137.5 meters. Use Δx = v₀t + ½at². Δx = (20 m/s * 5 s) + ½(3 m/s²)(5 s)².

8. A cheetah can accelerate from 0 to 27 m/s in 3 seconds. What distance does it cover in this time?

Show Answer

Answer: 40.5 meters. First find acceleration: a = 27/3 = 9 m/s². Then use Δx = v₀t + ½at². Δx = 0 + ½(9)(3)².

9. A driver sees an obstacle 80 m away and applies the brakes, causing a deceleration of 5 m/s². If the car was initially traveling at 30 m/s, does it stop in time?

Show Answer

Answer: No. The stopping distance is 90 meters. Use v² = v₀² + 2aΔx. (0² = 30² + 2(-5)Δx). 0 = 900 - 10Δx. Δx = 90 m.

10. What is the maximum height reached by the ball in question 5? (v₀ = 25 m/s)

Show Answer

Answer: 31.89 meters. Use v² = v₀² + 2aΔx. (0² = 25² + 2(-9.8)Δx). 0 = 625 - 19.6Δx.

Key Tips for Kinematics

1. Watch Your Signs

Establish a coordinate system. Up and right are usually positive; down and left are negative. Velocity, displacement, and acceleration must have signs that match your system. Deceleration is just acceleration in the opposite direction of velocity.

2. List Your Knowns

Before starting a problem, write down all the variables you know (v₀, v, a, t, Δx) and the one you need to find. This makes it much easier to choose the correct kinematic equation.

3. Master the Graphs

Understand the relationships: the slope of a position-time graph is velocity, and the slope of a velocity-time graph is acceleration. The area under a velocity-time graph is displacement.

4. Free Fall Facts

For any object in free fall (thrown or dropped), the acceleration is always 'g' (approx. 9.8 m/s² or 10 m/s²) directed downwards. The velocity at the highest point of an object's trajectory is momentarily zero.
