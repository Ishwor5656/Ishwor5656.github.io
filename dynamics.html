Dynamics

The study of why objects move the way they do, focusing on the forces that cause changes in motion.

The Causes of Motion

While kinematics describes motion, dynamics explains it.

The Heart of Dynamics: F_net = ma

Dynamics is built upon Newton's Second Law. It states that the acceleration (a) of an object is directly proportional to the net force (F_net) acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass (m). This simple equation is the bridge between the cause (force) and the effect (acceleration). If the net force on an object is zero, it will not accelerate, which is the essence of Newton's First Law.

Common Forces

  • Gravity (Weight): The force of attraction between two objects with mass. On Earth, your weight is the force of gravity pulling you down (W = mg).
  • Normal Force: The support force exerted by a surface on an object in contact with it. It's always perpendicular to the surface.
  • Friction: A force that opposes motion between surfaces in contact. It can be static (when at rest) or kinetic (when moving).
  • Tension: The pulling force transmitted through a string, rope, cable, or wire.

Free-Body Diagrams

The single most important tool in solving dynamics problems. A free-body diagram is a simplified drawing of an object showing all the forces acting *on* it. By isolating the object and representing forces as arrows, we can easily apply F_net = ma in different directions (e.g., x and y axes) to analyze its motion.

Test Your Knowledge

Analyze forces and solve for motion.

1. A heavy truck and a small car have a head-on collision. Which vehicle experiences the greater force of impact? Which experiences the greater acceleration?

Show Answer

Answer: According to Newton's Third Law, the forces are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction. However, due to F=ma, the car (with its much smaller mass) will experience a much greater acceleration (change in velocity).

2. If you are standing in an elevator that is accelerating upwards, do you feel heavier or lighter? Why?

Show Answer

Answer: You feel heavier. The floor must not only support your weight (mg) but also provide an additional upward force to accelerate you upwards. This larger normal force is what you perceive as your apparent weight.

3. Why is it harder to start pushing a heavy box from rest than it is to keep it sliding?

Show Answer

Answer: The coefficient of static friction (for objects at rest) is typically greater than the coefficient of kinetic friction (for moving objects). Therefore, a larger force is required to overcome static friction and get the box moving.

4. A book rests on a table. The table exerts an upward normal force on the book. What is the "reaction" force to this normal force according to Newton's Third Law?

Show Answer

Answer: The reaction force is the book exerting an equal and opposite downward force on the table. Action-reaction pairs always act on different objects.

5. Can the normal force on an object ever be greater than its weight?

Show Answer

Answer: Yes. For example, if you push down on a book resting on a table, the table must exert a normal force equal to the book's weight PLUS your downward push. Also, in an upward-accelerating elevator, the normal force is greater than weight.

6. If an object is moving at a constant velocity, what can you say about the net force acting on it?

Show Answer

Answer: The net force is zero. Constant velocity means zero acceleration. According to F_net = ma, if 'a' is zero, then 'F_net' must also be zero. The forces acting on it are balanced.

7. Imagine you are pulling a sled across snow. If you pull the rope at an upward angle, how does this affect the friction force between the sled and the snow?

Show Answer

Answer: It reduces the friction force. The upward component of your pulling force supports some of the sled's weight, which reduces the downward force on the snow. This, in turn, reduces the normal force, and since friction depends on the normal force, friction is also reduced.

8. Can an object be in motion and have no forces acting on it?

Show Answer

Answer: In a perfect scenario, yes. According to Newton's First Law, an object in motion stays in motion with constant velocity if the net force is zero. An asteroid moving through deep space far from any gravitational sources is a good example.

9. You tie a rock to a string and swing it in a vertical circle. At which point in the circle is the tension in the string the greatest?

Show Answer

Answer: At the bottom of the circle. At this point, the tension must not only counteract the rock's weight (gravity) but also provide the necessary upward centripetal force to keep it moving in a circle. The forces add together.

10. Does the mass of an object affect how fast it falls in a vacuum?

Show Answer

Answer: No. The force of gravity (weight) is greater on a more massive object, but its inertia (resistance to acceleration) is also greater. These two effects cancel out perfectly (a = F/m = mg/m = g), so all objects fall with the same acceleration 'g' in a vacuum.

1. A net force of 50 N is applied to a 10 kg box. What is the acceleration of the box?

Show Answer

Answer: 5 m/s². Use F_net = ma. a = F_net / m = 50 N / 10 kg.

2. A 70 kg person stands on a scale in an elevator accelerating downwards at 1.5 m/s². What does the scale read? (Use g = 9.8 m/s²)

Show Answer

Answer: 581 N. The net force is F_net = W - N = ma. So the normal force (scale reading) is N = W - ma = mg - ma = m(g - a) = 70 kg * (9.8 - 1.5) m/s².

3. A 20 kg crate is pushed with a horizontal force of 100 N. If the coefficient of kinetic friction is 0.2, what is the acceleration of the crate? (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Show Answer

Answer: 3 m/s². First, find the friction force: f = μN = μ(mg) = 0.2 * (20 kg * 10 m/s²) = 40 N. Then, F_net = F_push - f = 100 N - 40 N = 60 N. Finally, a = F_net / m = 60 N / 20 kg.

4. A car's engine provides a forward thrust of 5000 N. The car has a mass of 1200 kg and experiences a drag force of 800 N. What is the car's acceleration?

Show Answer

Answer: 3.5 m/s². The net force is F_net = F_thrust - F_drag = 5000 N - 800 N = 4200 N. Then, a = F_net / m = 4200 N / 1200 kg.

5. What is the weight of an 80 kg astronaut on Mars, where the gravitational acceleration is 3.7 m/s²?

Show Answer

Answer: 296 N. Weight is the force of gravity, W = mg = 80 kg * 3.7 m/s².

6. A force of 10 N is required to pull a 5 kg block at a constant velocity over a horizontal surface. What is the coefficient of kinetic friction?

Show Answer

Answer: 0.204. Constant velocity means F_net = 0. So, F_pull = f = 10 N. We also know f = μN = μ(mg). Therefore, μ = f / (mg) = 10 N / (5 kg * 9.8 m/s²).

7. Two masses, 5 kg and 3 kg, are connected by a string over a frictionless pulley (Atwood machine). What is the acceleration of the system?

Show Answer

Answer: 2.45 m/s². The net force is the difference in weights: F_net = (5kg*g) - (3kg*g). The total mass being accelerated is M_total = 5kg + 3kg. So, a = F_net / M_total = (2kg * 9.8m/s²) / 8kg.

8. A 1500 kg car accelerates from 10 m/s to 30 m/s in 5 seconds. What is the net force on the car?

Show Answer

Answer: 6000 N. First find acceleration: a = (v - v₀) / t = (30 - 10) / 5 = 4 m/s². Then use F_net = ma = 1500 kg * 4 m/s².

9. A 2 kg book is pushed against a vertical wall with a horizontal force of 40 N. If the coefficient of static friction is 0.6, will the book slide down?

Show Answer

Answer: No. The normal force is equal to the push, N = 40 N. The maximum static friction is f_max = μN = 0.6 * 40 N = 24 N. The book's weight is W = mg = 2 kg * 9.8 m/s² = 19.6 N. Since the max friction (24 N) is greater than the weight (19.6 N), the book will not slide.

10. What is the tension in the string for the Atwood machine in question 7?

Show Answer

Answer: 36.75 N. Look at the 3 kg mass: F_net = T - m₂g = m₂a. So, T = m₂(g + a) = 3 kg * (9.8 + 2.45) m/s². (You get the same answer analyzing the 5 kg mass).

Key Tips for Dynamics

1. Draw the Free-Body Diagram!

This is not optional. Always start by drawing the object and all the forces acting on it as arrows pointing away from the center. This visual tool is essential for correctly identifying and summing forces.

2. Net Force is the Goal

Your first step in any calculation is to find the net force along the direction of motion. Sum up all the forces, making sure to get the signs right based on your chosen coordinate system. Remember: F_net = ma, not just any single force.

3. Choose Axes Wisely

For problems on an incline, it's almost always easiest to tilt your x-y coordinate system so that the x-axis is parallel to the slope and the y-axis is perpendicular to it. This simplifies the forces and acceleration.

4. Action-Reaction Pairs Act on Different Bodies

Never put an action-reaction pair on the same free-body diagram. The force of a box on a table and the force of the table on the box act on two different objects (the table and the box, respectively).
