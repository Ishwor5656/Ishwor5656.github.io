Classical Mechanics

The foundational branch of physics concerned with the motion of bodies under the action of forces.

Core Concepts

The essential principles that govern the motion of everyday objects.

Newton's Laws of Motion

Three fundamental laws: inertia, F=ma, and action-reaction. They form the basis for understanding how forces affect motion.

Kinematics

The description of motion without considering its causes. It deals with concepts like displacement, velocity, and acceleration.

Dynamics

The study of the causes of motion and changes in motion. It connects forces with the resulting kinematic quantities.

Work and Energy

The principle that energy is conserved. Work is the transfer of energy, relating to kinetic and potential energy.

Momentum and Collisions

The concept of momentum (mass in motion) and its conservation, which is crucial for analyzing collisions between objects.

Rotational Motion

The motion of objects rotating around an axis. It involves concepts like torque, angular velocity, and moment of inertia.

The World of the Predictable

Classical mechanics, often called Newtonian mechanics after Sir Isaac Newton, is one of the oldest and most familiar parts of physics. It excels at describing the motion of macroscopic objectsâ€”everything from a thrown ball to the orbits of planets. Its principles are so accurate for everyday scales that they are fundamental to almost every field of engineering and technology.

The beauty of classical mechanics lies in its deterministic nature. If you know the initial conditions of a system (like the position and velocity of an object) and the forces acting on it, you can, in principle, predict its entire future trajectory. This predictable, clockwork view of the universe dominated scientific thought for centuries until the advent of relativity and quantum mechanics revealed its limitations at very high speeds and very small scales.
